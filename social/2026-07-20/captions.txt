=== POST 1: india-turn ===
The world's jewelry factory just restarted. 🇮🇳

India's gems and jewellery exports jumped 26.5% in June to $2.21 BILLION. Gold jewellery did the heavy lifting — up 54.5% — but the number that matters is quieter: cut-and-polished diamond exports rose 8.7%, the category's first clean monthly gain after a slump that has closed mines on other continents.

The fine print keeps it honest: the full quarter was flat, and the average exported carat now sells for just $682 — smaller, cheaper goods are doing the recovering. Work is back. Margin isn't. Yet.

=== POST 2: gold-floor ===
Gold's $4,000 floor has been hit three times — and held three times. The fourth test comes with a Fed meeting attached. 📉

Friday looked like the break: spot traded down to $3,959.30, five dollars off the triple bottom every technician is watching. Then the late save — a $42 rally into the close at $4,017.30, and a Monday open at $4,018.

The tug-of-war: oil at $90 feeds inflation, inflation feeds rate-hike odds (December now ~82%), and higher rates tax gold. Meanwhile silver is quietly doing the work — up 1.67% Monday morning while gold argues with the Fed.

=== POST 3: botswana-cards ===
Anglo American finally picked a buyer for De Beers. Botswana's answer: nice choice — we'll decide. 👑

On Friday, minister Moeti Mohwasa told parliament that Botswana's 15% stake comes with 'complete freedom': join the winning consortium as a partner, exercise its right of first refusal ALONE, or bring in a third party of its own choosing.

The preferred bidder is a reunion: Gareth Penny — De Beers' own CEO from 2006 to 2010 — leading a consortium with a Qatari fund and Israeli businessman Nir Livnat, with Angola and Namibia eyed as participants. Two bidders remain from six. Close is targeted by Q4.

But diamonds are ~80% of Botswana's exports and a third of state revenue. Every path to closing runs through Gaborone.

=== POST 4: alrosa-green ===
The most carbon-neutral diamonds in the world are the ones you're not allowed to buy. 🤔

TÜV Austria has certified ALL 29.8 million carats of Alrosa's 2025 production carbon-neutral — second year running. The engineering is real: 76% renewable electricity, petroleum swapped for gas, and kimberlite ore that absorbs CO2 as it weathers. Net result: the Russian miner's operations absorbed 126,000 tonnes MORE carbon than they emitted.

And none of it moves a single stone through Antwerp — G7 sanctions have barred Russian diamonds from Western markets since 2024.

So here's the debate: natural diamond marketing sells provenance and planet as one bundled promise. What happens when the loudest green credential belongs to the producer the West locked out? Does carbon math matter at the counter — or does origin beat everything? Tell us below. 👇

=== POST 5: ruby-tape ===
Rubies just got a price tag everyone can see: $66.30 a carat, wholesale. 💎❤️

The colored-stone trade has always envied one thing about diamonds — a printed price. Gemfields' new 'Trade Select' auction is building one for the middle of the ruby market: 348,409 carats sold (93.2% of everything offered), $23.1 million total, with sapphire categories debuting alongside for the first time.

Why it matters at the counter: a transparent clearing price for honest mid-grade rough is the raw material of the $1,500–$5,000 colored-stone case — exactly where color has been taking share from diamonds all year.

Every maturing market eventually splits its tape into grades. Color is finally doing what bullion did generations ago.
