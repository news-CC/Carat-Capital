The Apollo Gallery reopens, minus the crown jewels

Nine months after thieves took over $100 million in French royal jewels in a seven-minute raid, the Louvre's Apollo Gallery reopened July 22. The jewels will not return to it. Four suspects are in custody; the stones are still gone.

By The Security Desk · 2026-07-24 · 4 min read
The raid · as recorded
Where
Apollo Gallery, Musée du Louvre, Paris
When
October 2025 · reopened 22 July 2026
Taken
French royal jewels, $100M+
Method
Basket lift · angle grinder · ~7 minutes

Recovered
None

§1The room reopened; the jewels did not.

The Louvre reopened its Apollo Gallery to the public on July 22, nine months after the October 2025 heist that turned the most famous jewel room in the world into a crime scene. Visitors filed back beneath the gilded ceiling to find the cases that once held the French crown jewels empty. The museum has decided the gallery will no longer display royal jewellery at all; the surviving pieces are being moved to a more secure location, and the Apollo reverts to its older function as a state gallery.

The raid it is recovering from was a study in speed. A crew disguised as construction workers parked a truck-mounted basket lift against the building's river façade, rode it to a first-floor window, cut through with an angle grinder and a disc cutter, lifted jewelled pieces valued at more than $100 million from their cases, and were back on motorcycles in roughly seven minutes. It was less a burglary than a logistics exercise, executed against a national museum in daylight.

§2Seven minutes against a national museum.

The investigation has produced arrests but no jewels. Four suspects are in custody; by the account reported this week, they say they were hired hands and have declined to name whoever commissioned the theft. No stones have been recovered, and the working assumption across the trade is grim: pieces this recognisable are worth far more broken and recut than intact, which means the French crown jewels may already have ceased to exist as the objects the catalogue describes.

§3The bill lands on everyone who lends a stone.

The reopening doubles as a policy moment. France's culture ministry has announced a national plan to harden security at museums and national collections, an admission that the vulnerability was systemic rather than local. The art-recovery specialist Christopher Marinello put the lesson bluntly: if thieves can get into the Louvre, they can get in anywhere. For a jewellery trade that leans on museums to display, authenticate and lend prestige to historic stones, that is an uncomfortable sentence to sit under.

The Louvre's real decision this week was not to reopen the room but to empty it, and that is the more honest response. A gallery that cannot protect the crown jewels should not hold them, and moving them to a vault concedes what nine months of investigation confirmed, that display and security had drifted too far apart. The wider bill lands on every institution that borrows historic jewels to draw a crowd; insurers and lenders will now price the Apollo raid into every loan agreement.

Prestige, it turns out, has a security deposit.
