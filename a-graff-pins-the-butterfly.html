Graff pins the butterfly: twelve brooches built to tremble

A new high-jewelry capsule revives the 17th-century jabot pin with en tremblant wings, Paraíba tourmalines, invisibly set pink sapphires and a house motif dating to 1975. Every piece converts to a pendant. Prices on request.

By The Design Desk · 2026-07-22 · 4 min read
The capsule · what Graff built
PIECES
12 brooches, each convertible to a pendant
FORMAT
Jabot pin — the 17th-century double-ended fastener
MECHANICS
En tremblant wings that move with the wearer
STONES
Paraíba tourmaline, emerald, yellow diamond, invisible-set pink sapphire
MOTIF SINCE
1975 — the house butterfly, symbol of transformation
PRICE
On request

§1A 1975 motif, re-pinned.

Graff has taken the butterfly, its house motif since 1975, and pinned it to one of jewelry's oldest formats. The London house's new high-jewelry capsule comprises twelve brooches designed as jabot pins, the double-ended fasteners of the seventeenth century, each one convertible into a pendant. The wings are mounted en tremblant, on tiny sprung mechanisms, so the butterflies flutter with the wearer's movement rather than sitting still under glass.

§2The full register of stones.

The stone work runs the house's full register. Tamara sets a marquise-cut emerald at the center of an Art Deco sunburst in white gold and diamonds. Freya borrows an archival dragonfly design and paves its wings in fancy-cut and pear-shaped yellow diamonds. Eva, the painterly one, arcs a gradient of sapphires, emeralds, Paraíba tourmalines and diamonds across its span, an effect the house traces to Monet's garden canvases, with miniature butterflies scattered around the principal one for extra motion. Lina works square-cut pink sapphires into an invisible setting, finishing the jabot tip with a marquise pink sapphire and a pavé butterfly.

Design director Anne-Eva Geffroy framed the collection simply: "there is something deeply poetic about the form of a butterfly". The poetry has structure under it. The jabot format solves the brooch's oldest commercial problem, which is that modern wardrobes give it nowhere to sit; a pin that becomes a pendant is two objects for one price, and the tip ornament, a single butterfly, emblem or jewel at the pin's opposite end, lets the piece read differently on a lapel, a knit or a gown.

All twelve pieces are priced on request, which at Graff's level of stone sourcing is less coyness than honesty: no two Paraíba gradients cost the same.
— The Design Desk

§3The brooch revival has numbers.

The capsule lands in a brooch market that is quietly reviving. Auction bidders have spent two seasons paying attention to convertible and mechanical jewels, the couture houses put monumental brooches on half the fall runways, and en tremblant work, a nineteenth-century specialty, has become shorthand for the kind of handwork machine settings cannot fake. All twelve pieces are priced on request, which at Graff's level of stone sourcing is less coyness than honesty: no two Paraíba gradients cost the same.

The brooch has been declared dead more often than the mechanical watch, and it keeps returning for the same reason: it is the only jewel that does not need a body part to justify it. Graff betting twelve high-jewelry pieces on jabot pins is a statement that the top of the market wants objects with mechanics, history and a second life.

The trade should note which of the twelve sells first: if it is Eva, the Paraíba gradient, the color market's fever has officially reached the brooch tray.
