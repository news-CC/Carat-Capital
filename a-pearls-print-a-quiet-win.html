Four times the estimate: the pearl keeps proving the trade wrong

Tenoris counts pearl jewelry among the surprises of America's first half; at Bonhams London this month, a two-row natural strand made £39,680 against a £10,000 estimate. Retail data and the auction floor are telling the same story.

By The Color Desk · 2026-07-21 · 4 min read

§1Two ledgers agree.

Two very different ledgers agreed about pearls this month. In Tenoris's first-half review of American jewelry retail — a half in which overall unit sales fell and growth leaned on ever-higher tickets — pearl jewelry stood out as a surprising strength, a category posting a solid run without the benefit of a bridal occasion or a metals tape to drive it. Days earlier, the auction market had said the same thing with a hammer.

Days earlier, the auction market had said the same thing with a hammer.
— The Color Desk

§2The hammer confirms.

At Bonhams' London jewels sale on July 8, a two-row natural pearl necklace with a diamond clasp brought £39,680, roughly $53,250, against an estimate of £10,000 — four times the low expectation. A David Morris cultured pearl and diamond necklace made £40,960 over a £25,000–35,000 estimate. "Natural pearls, colored gemstones, signed, and period jewelry all performed strongly," said Jennifer Tonkin, Bonhams' head of jewelry in the UK, in JCK's account of the sale — results this page reported at the time, now looking less like one good afternoon and more like a pattern.

§3A ladder customers can climb.

The design desks got there first. Roseate's new Odyssey collection — pearls carrying luminous, hope-laden motifs, covered admiringly by the trade press this month — is one of a string of launches treating the pearl as a first-choice gem rather than an heirloom afterthought. The category's case writes itself in 2026: an organic gem with no per-ounce tape attached, priced below the gold-and-diamond arms race, with a supply story at the natural end that auction results keep validating.

There is a hierarchy inside the win. Cultured goods are carrying the retail volume; natural pearls, finite by definition, are carrying the records. The space between those two prices — a few hundred dollars to fifty thousand — gives the pearl something almost nothing else in the showcase has right now: a ladder a customer can actually climb.
